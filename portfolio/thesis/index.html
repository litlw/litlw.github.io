Honeybot Experiment

by Hugh Ritchie

Intro

I wanted to build a machine that would be the subject of the attacks from the web. These attacks could actually show people what is going on. I think that a huge first step for teaching about what goes on “in the dark” is to just show it to them. I wanted to find a way to do that.

First, I looked into a web manipulator, but almost immediately realized that it was too much of a risk on my computer and my personal network

I consulted some coworkers who have significantly more experience in this field than myself. Thomas Jack, a network analyst and enthusiast for data security and privacy; and Brendan Vincelli, a talented developer and enthusiast for open source and the open data movement. Almost immediately they were excited to be a part of this experiment.

Quickly they explained to me that a web browser going around acting naively is not actually a good example of how data privacy is breached or broken. A much better example would be to show how a server gets attacked.

Brendan showed me a tool called “Damn Vulnerable Web App”, which is a training tool for network security people to practice their hacking skills in a safe environment.

The webapp is itself vulnerable, and thus I decided it would be a good guineapig. I went to a hosting site called “Digital Ocean” similar to AWS or google servers, but more hands-off (to allow hacking to occur). A server on Digital Ocean is pretty cheap, at about 22 cents per hour, and is referred to as a droplet. I set up my droplet and began immediately to prepare for the extraction of data.

My plan: Collect all the syslogs, copy the syslogs to a google drive folder of a falsified account on google (for security purposes). Set Cron to run the copy script every 20 minutes (No idea how often I would get hacked, or how long until someone broke the machine). As a final measure, make snapshots of the machine to ensure that I can “roll back” to a previous version in the case that someone had a “Home Run” and my computer’s root access was taken from me.

With script in hand and a vulnerable server I was set to go. But as soon as I started I visited the server itself. This wasn’t a fake app or a fake website, like I had hoped it would be (like a hosted store where someone could try their hand at stealing data). This was a training terminal. Disappointed, I shut down the droplet and went back to the drawing board.

I locked down everything and went searching for a decent way to capture data about malicious online activity. After all my reading I was certain that there is something already made. I don’t need my machine to be destroyed, I just want to see what people try to do.

By a stroke of luck in my research, I came upon an open source repository made by T Mobile. I heard that developers from many European companies (especially infosec) had been encouraged to write code and contribute to the open source community. This must be one of those projects, I thought to myself. It did not have very much documentation, and it used other well-known open source tools (Including my favourite, Elasticsearch).

I downloaded and read through the files in the repository and set out to install it on my machine. I was hesitant at first – since it seemed that this would completely erase my work thus far on my virtual machine. Not only that, but it could also get me banned. Generally, these hosting sites don’t want malicious activity on their servers. I took the risk anyways.

The installation process was difficult, and once the machine was installed and prepared, it didn’t work. It seems that in order for this to work, I would need to allocate more resources. I shut off the VM, increased my resource pool, and booted it up again. Success! The machine was alive and well!

Three hours later It was down again. Hacked – defeated. The honeypot had been got. I was devastated, frantically trying to get access to my creation again. When I did get online I saw I had an uninvited visitor, and in a panic I immediately disconnected and erased the machine. I started the whole process again, but this time with a seriously tough password. It was generated out of a hashing program, and it is super long. From what I have learned about brute force algorithms, this would take at least a month to crack – more than enough time to blacklist an attacker.

With the machine up and running for 48 hours I was blown away by the numbers. My machine had been attacked ONE MILLION TIMES. I leaned back in my chair and took a deep breath. It was unbelievable. Most of it was from china, of course. As I peeled deeper into the data, however , I saw so many surprising sources. So many corporations and organizations were tied to these attacks.

Of course, those companies are likely uninvolved. A hacker might just use those DNS names to hide their own identity. It’s unlikely a botnet would actually have its owner’s information attached to the headers of the attack. No one is that dumb.

Hypothesis

Duration

I hypothesize that the machine will run for 4 Days before it is either overloaded with traffic or brought down by hackers.

From what I have been reading, the biggest thing to watch out for is a "home run" - which basically means losing control of your computer because a hacker has found an unstoppable way of getting in.

Number Of Attacks

My hypothesis in this experiment is that my honeypot will be attacked about 100,000 Times. I know there are lots of players on the web, and that the hacking community is huge. That being said, I am not advertising my machine and I don’t know if it could even handle more traffic.

From what I have read on the T-Pot site, the machine should be resilient enough to handle shutdowns, and temporary outages. That is good because I’m certain that DigitalOcean does regular backups that will probably disrupt my machine.

Source Of Attacks

Also, from what I learned while reading Black Code, I also expect that most of these attacks will be coming from non-north American sources.

My best guess, based on the ‘ghostnet’ scandal, as well as the recent news about Trump winning the election, is that the top countries will be either Russia or China. That is not a statement about those people, it is just a statement about the names of countries I have been hearing in the news associated with the recent data attacks.

Types Of Attacks

I don’t have much to say about the types of attacks. I suspect the most common attack will be from the 'Cowrie' honeypot - which watches for SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol) attacks.

The reason why those will be the most common is that they are the least invasive. Someone can do an SNMP call on a machine from anywhere with little resistance. What that does is it returns the information about the machine – such as who is connected to it, where it is on the web, and what the machine is. It’s like a diagnostic report that can be pulled.

The other more invasive attacks will happen as well, but I hypothesize that the attacks will be 80% Non-Invasive and 20% Invasive attacks.

Results

Duration

In my hypothesis, I proposed that the machine would run for 4 days. It ran for 3 days. Just after the three day mark, the machine crashed. Because of the nature of the machine setup I was using, I can’t see what made the machine go down. (I can’t turn the machine on without connecting it to the internet)

I was not hit by a home run - I was hit by a massive swarm of attacks.

Number Of Attacks

I hypothesized that the machine would be hit 100,000 times. The final number, however, was 1,016,060 times. More than 100 times my original hypothesis.

If these attacks were flies buzzing around you, they would block out your view of the sun - they would stop you from being able to breathe.

What was really interesting was that the attacks did not come in at an even rate. From what I understood from my research, most attacks would come from constantly running scripts that hackers would setup and deploy.

What happened instead is a serious increase in traffic after the first 24 hours. It feels like once I was online for long enough, the number of attackers increased - or the number of scripts they used to attack me increased.

Source Of Attacks

The source of the attacks, it turns out, doesn’t matter. The reason I say this is that most of the attacks came from DigitalOcean - the same service I was using to run this honeypot. What that tells me is most hackers are using digital hosting services to carry out their activity.

While normally I would say that the hosting companies should review what their machines are actually doing - it’s the same blindness that allowed me to do this project in the first place. It’s a hard balance to strike.

One thing was for sure - the attacks were largely coming from America / American companies.

Types Of Attacks

I was bang-on with the types of attacks. The overwhelming majority of attacks came from the Cowrie Honeypot, which watches for SNMP Attacks. I am not surprised by this result - considering that the SNMP Protocol is a common first step to investigating a computer.

It’s something that companies use to investigate their own machines when something goes wrong. That being said, using SNMP to creep around someone else’s machine is trespassing.

The relative difference between the SNMP attacks and the SSH attacks was approximately a 10 to 1, while the other more complex attacks were closer a 100 to 1.

Conclusion

I learned in this exercise that there are significantly more attackers on the web than I had expected. My hypothetical numbers were blown out by so much and in such a short time. Not only that, but the short lifespan of my honeypots was just a reminder that the security I set up for any future projects should really be tight.

I know that there are standards of practice like changing your ssh port and creating barriers to keep people out, but that still leaves hundreds of attacks that would have found another way in.

I wanted to explore this space so that I could show people what was happening in the web. What I found instead was that I was not even prepared for the numbers I found.

The single largest shock in reviewing the results was that the United States was by far the most common place for hackers to originate from. I also noticed that the service I used to host my own honeypot was the service that hosted a lot of these machines. I guess that means that hackers have begun to use these services to run their own projects?

I haven’t had a chance to peel through the data of what the hackers did once inside the machine, but I am certain it’s not good stuff. I come away from this experience with confidence that the average computer is not secure. I wanted to encourage others to think about privacy and to really consider whether their own app providers were secure, but now I am considering that for myself.

Imagine how often google gets attacked – imagine how viciously and constant those attacks must me.

Background

How aware are you of data privacy as an issue? How does technological literacy relate to that awareness? People tend to trust their apps because of a social investment in the technology. In Germany, for instance, a study was done with 674 responding student participants mostly from the University of Salzburg was conducted (Fuchs, 177). This study featured a series of questions presented to both undergraduate and graduate students to assess their knowledge or understanding of data privacy. The results of the study showed that 16.5% of students had zero of potential four correct answers, implying no understanding of data privacy and surveillance; 65.3% had one, implying a very low level of understanding; 16.5% had two correct answers; and 1.8% had all three correct answers, showing a good understanding of data privacy and surveillance (Fuchs, 178).

In 2013, a European study called Eurobarometer was conducted with 16,244 EU citizens from several countries (Sobolewski, 211). In that group, 80% felt not in control over their own personal data. Of that 80%, 66% felt concerned about that fact (Sobolewski, 211). While that suggests an interest in their own data privacy, that same study showed that these participants were willing to hand over their personal data in exchange for very little compensation (Sobolewski, 212). Eurobarometer conducted an adjacent study which found that Europeans are not taking the simplest actions to protect themselves, such as changing their privacy settings on their social media platforms (Sobolewski, 212). This is referred to as the “Privacy Paradox” (Sobolewski, 212).

In the wake of massive data leaks from Facebook, Ashley Madison, Target, and Equifax, users are clearly concerned about their data privacy (Fairclough, 1). Users might not take action to protect themselves – but the users also might not know enough to be able to take action. Users clearly want more protection, more privacy, and more security. That being said, they might not even know how to ask for more privacy.


Council of Europe Data Privacy Convention (Greenleaf, 68). The groundwork set by these two large pieces of legislature would eventually grow into a cohesive data privacy plan that would inspire countries around the world to follow suit (Greenleaf, 70). The number of data privacy laws enforced in the EU grew from the 1980’s through to today. In 1970, there were 8 data privacy laws; in the 1980’s, 13 new laws were introduced; in the 2000’s, 21 new laws were introduced; and between 2010 and 2012, 12 new laws had been introduced (Greenleaf, 69).

Of those new laws, was the Information Privacy Act (IPA), which focused on Health Education and Welfare of European people (Greenleaf, 71). The IPA plainly stated, “Data collected for one purpose should not be used or made available for other purposes without consent" (Greenleaf, 71). The Data Protection Directive, which later produced the Data Protection Agency (DPA), added significantly stronger protections to Data Privacy (Greenleaf, 68). The DPA today is part of the EU’s international Data policy when discussing data. The rules that the DPA laid out for a country to be considered to have sufficient data privacy laws in place are as follows: Does that country have a national law that monitors public and private sectors; are there basic data privacy principles (according to OECD Guielines); and is there a statutory enforcement for these laws (Greenleaf, 69).

From a privacy and security standpoint, Convention 108 from the Council of Europe is the most significant addition to a legal corpus supporting users. Convention 108 is “the convention for the protection of individuals with regard to automatic processing of personal data” (Greenleaf, 81). In other words, protections against growing Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence methods of data processing. In addition to machine learning, Convention 108 seeks to limit the countries where that data can go. It would behave a lot like border security for data in and out of the EU and supported countries. Not allowing specific traffic in or out of these countries on a base legal level means that the ability for producers of data insights and machine learning tools, which house our personal identifiable data, to defend against and prosecute breaches of privacy is enhanced significantly.

'Black Code' by Ronald Deibert goes much more in depth to various other kinds of espionage and sale of data, which is interesting as a reader, but not to my thesis. The book is a recount of deibert’s lab, ‘Citizen Labs’ which investigates activity on the web that is kept from our view. His lab receives lots of criticism from governments, scholars, and corporations – and yet it also is simply the lab trying to learn what they can about the web. What was so interesting about this book was not only the massive conspiracies the lab uncovered, but the degree to which governments don’t want Deibert to be investigating or exposing the activity on the web.

In terms of the display of the information that I gathered, I turn to the grandfathers of Data Visualization. Edward Tufte and Jaques Bertin are the two most prolific researchers in this field – laying out the forms and structures, basic principles, and best practices of the Visual Representation of data.

In Tufte’s book, ‘The Visual Display of Quantitative Information’, Tufte delivers an encyclopedic tome of visualization rules and definitions. These Definitions include the eight visual variables: Size, Colour, Value, Orientation, Shape , Position, Pattern, and Texture. He also sets up the rules of what makes an ‘efficient’ visualization.

In Bertin’s book, ‘Semiology of Graphics, Diagrams, Networks, Maps’, the focus for Bertin is on the levels of reading a visualization. Bertin provides the framework for visual stimuli, broken into three categories: Monosemic, Polysemic, and Pansemic. He follows up by explaining the three levels of reading or understanding a visualization: Elementary, Intermediate and Overall. These three levels are more like stages rather than actual levels, but they make testing for efficiency much easier.

I want to explore some of these tools for visualization in my thesis.

Timeline

March

Leading up to the end of thesis, I focused on visualizing the results of my honeypot. The spider exhibit idea didn’t go as well as I had hoped. What I did instead is I set up a screenshot script on my ubuntu computer at home, and let it run (making sure it didn’t fall asleep).

I designed my own version of the dashboard on the Kibana Server, and let the machine do it’s thing. With the Kibana server running, and my computer taking screenshots, I could make a time-lapse of the growth of the honeypot.

The time lapse came out looking amazing - with some hiccups. The machine crashed pretty quickly, which I was a little surprised by. I checked the status of the machine several days later, and the stats showed that it was running fine - but when I went to see how the machine was doing via the web portal, it was not starting up.

I turned that time lapse into a more narrative driven structure, and made some videos. That seemed the best way to show what was going on. This was interesting data to visualize - and the reason why is the data is most valuable while moving.

Generally with data visualization, data is valuable in its own perspective - insights can be gathered and value can be delivered from those insights. With this data, the insights were tough to see - and the time-lapse revealed why. This is a visualization of attacks from attackers. Attacks are most interesting to observe as they happen - and this play-by-play of attacks is exactly that.

    Ronald Deibert, the head of Citizen Labs in Toronto, has a long and prolific history of involvement with data privacy. He and his lab were recommended to me for research by one of my advisors, and what I have learned from them and about them has been incredible even after just a few weeks of reading.

    Deibert’s book is a story of his lab’s journeys through the discovery and undoing of China’s ghostnet, as well as other journeys. On top of that, it is a critique and opinion piece from Deibert about data privacy overall. His book thus far has helped me position my thoughts in my own thesis. My direction of “What happens to you when you are compromised” as an introductory learning piece for everyday users is definitely inspired by what happened with the ghostnet story, as well as other stories that I read about in the news.

    The basic thesis is simple. Hackers rarely hack people for their identity – they hack for access, for mechanical resource, or for widespread intelligence gathering. The difference between access and widespread intelligence gathering is the precision with which a hacker might act. For access, a hacker would target someone specifically so that they can get closer to their true target. This often happens on a personal device or on a personal network of devices or area networks. Widespread intelligence gathering, on the other hand, is for the purpose of monitoring the consensus of devices over a very large network of people. This is often less invasive and also often less damaging to its victims. These two methods, however, are not mutually exclusive and can be part of the same strategy.

    Long story short – this article gave me focus.

What was also clear was that the exchange of goods or services for a person’s data was a very uneven scale. The average user is / was giving away their precious identifiable data in exchange for nearly insignificant conveniences on their mobile devices.

  • The New York Times. “How Cambridge Analytica Exploited the Facebook Data of Millions | NYT.” Youtube, The New York Times, April 9, 2018, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mrnXv-g4yKU.

    In an effort to keep up with the current times, I often turn to New York Times and Globe and Mail for resources. This video in particular covers the events that took place over an agreement between Cambridge Analytica and Facebook. This event, which is still shaking the world with respect to data privacy, is exactly what I want to combat. The entire documentation that New York Times provides – while 100% accurate – is indicitave of what people think is the real end point of data privacy being compromised. There is some discussion in the other academic work of the adversary and the researcher as two ethical poles of people who need data.

    This article discusses neither of them in depth, and simply says “oops we spilled your data”. Cambridge analytica poses itself to be a researcher – benign and in the interest of overall social growth – while in reality we have no idea whom else might have wanted to purchase data from that company – by way of the university research lab.

    I want to dissuade people from thinking a facebook data leak is the real risk. It is not. The real risk is what people might do to accomplish their nefarious goals – and what they might use you for in the process.
